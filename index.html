What the Right Is Saying

  • House conservatives revolt over DHS deal: Right-wing media led with fury over the Senate's late-night vote to end the DHS shutdown. Fox News reports that House conservatives view the deal as a capitulation, while National Review's editors frame the funding crisis as emblematic of a broader inability to enforce spending discipline. The Washington Examiner tallied winners and losers, suggesting the Senate deal shortchanges immigration enforcement priorities.
  • Meta social media verdict sparks pushback: After a landmark California jury found Meta liable for addicting children, National Review argued the jury got it wrong, calling the verdict a threat to free speech and innovation. Fox News covered Meta's vow to "aggressively" fight the ruling. Meanwhile, AOC's call for politicians to reject "AI money" drew conservative scorn as tech-sector overreach.
  • Scott Bessent and trade policy: The WSJ editorial board profiled Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent as the administration's steadiest hand, arguing he can navigate the tension between Trump's tariff instincts and market stability. A separate WSJ opinion piece warned that Trump's lumber tariffs are directly raising the cost of disaster recovery, particularly for California fire rebuilding.
  • RFK Jr. declared a public health danger: A pointed WSJ editorial called RFK Jr. "dangerous to public health," citing his Senate hearing performance and his empowerment of trial lawyers over sound vaccine science — a notable rebuke from the paper's conservative opinion page.
  • Higher education probes: Conservative outlets largely cheered the Trump administration investigations into Stanford, Ohio State, and UCSD medical schools over admissions policies. National Review connected the university crackdown to broader security vulnerabilities, arguing that ideological capture of elite institutions is itself a national security issue.
  • Redistricting battles in Virginia and California: The Federalist ran multiple pieces arguing Virginia Democrats are sweating over an April redistricting referendum, while Axios reported that Barack Obama personally urged Newsom on California's Prop 50 redistricting push — a story the right frames as evidence of continued Democratic gerrymandering ambitions.
  • Kristi Noem's departure and DHS leadership: The Examiner covered Vance hosting his first White House fraud meeting, while the broader question of who now leads DHS — after Noem's departure — remains a major topic across right-leaning media, with the implication that her successor, reportedly Markwayne Mullin, will accelerate deportations.

What the Left Is Saying

  • "Trump is losing the Iran war": The Nation's lead analysis argues bluntly that Trump's shifting deadlines and inability to secure a resolution mean he is losing the Iran conflict on both military and diplomatic fronts. Vox dissected the moving deadlines, arguing that each postponement erodes credibility. The economic consequences — food supply bottlenecks, diesel spikes, grocery inflation — dominate progressive framing of the war as an avoidable catastrophe.
  • Democracy in crisis — and progressive media's own reckoning: The Nation published a sweeping piece on the "rapid and unprecedented collapse of American democracy," citing democratic backsliding metrics. But in a self-critical companion essay, The Nation also critiqued the "bad-news echo chamber" of pro-democracy Substack, arguing that progressive media is better at alarming people than organizing them. The Atlantic's piece on Hungary as the "first post-reality political campaign" drew explicit parallels to U.S. trends.
  • Mass deportations poised to accelerate after Noem: The Atlantic argues that Kristi Noem's departure from DHS clears the way for the true mass deportation apparatus, with her successor expected to be far more aggressive. The LA Times revealed internal DHS emails showing a DHS attorney said agents in Los Angeles should have "started hitting" protesters — a damning disclosure likely to fuel legal challenges.
  • Meta verdict as gateway for AI litigation: The Nation frames the landmark Meta/YouTube liability verdict not just as a win for child safety advocates but as opening the floodgates for AI-related litigation. Separately, Vox warned that the push toward "agentic AI" is entering genuinely dangerous territory, while the White House's own AI rollout is exposing internal rifts.
  • Higher education under siege: Progressive outlets covered the Trump administration's investigations into Stanford, Ohio State, and UCSD medical schools as the latest in a systematic campaign to bully universities into compliance. Jewish faculty at Harvard spoke out against the attacks, complicating the administration's narrative that the crackdowns are about combating antisemitism. The Atlantic reports the assault has hit legal snags, with courts blocking some funding threats.
  • Immigration and Trump's crackdown: The Nation detailed how Trump's immigration crackdown extends well beyond undocumented immigrants, devastating international students and the universities that depend on their tuition — particularly in Illinois. New Jersey legislation barring local ICE contracts and mandating mask removal by agents was also a major progressive talking point.
  • Sudan atrocities continue unseen: The Guardian reported that two drone strikes on civilian targets in Sudan killed 28 people, hitting a market in North Darfur and a truck in North Kordofan. Progressive media continues to frame Sudan as the world's most neglected humanitarian catastrophe.
