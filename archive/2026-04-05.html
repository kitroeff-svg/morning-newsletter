Sunday, April 05, 2026

The U.S.-Iran conflict dominates this Easter Sunday: the second crew member from the F-15E Strike Eagle shot down over Iran has been rescued by American forces, even as Trump issued a 48-hour ultimatum threatening Iran with "hell" if the Strait of Hormuz isn't reopened — a threat The Atlantic calls the product of a catastrophic intelligence failure. Iran has escalated by striking Kuwait's water, power, and government infrastructure. On the home front, Stephen Miller is quietly pressing ahead on immigration enforcement as a wrenching NYT investigation documents Texas children left to raise siblings after ICE detained their parents, and a federal judge has blocked the Trump administration's demand that colleges in 17 states hand over student race and admissions data. In Gaza, Palestinians are streaming back to the devastated north on foot after a hostage-release breakthrough, while freed Israeli hostages are reported to still carry shrapnel from the Oct. 7 attack.

What the Right Is Saying

  • The Iran war as a test of resolve, not a failure: National Review's "The Fog of War Against Iran" frames the conflict as messy but necessary, arguing the U.S. must not flinch despite setbacks like the downed F-15. A companion NR piece argues the conflict exposes dangerous gaps in the West's military-industrial base and calls for urgent defense-sector investment.
  • Trump's military-first budget: Axios reports Trump is betting his presidency on the Pentagon, slashing domestic spending to fund the Iran campaign. The right-leaning WSJ editorial board backs Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, arguing he can navigate the resulting trade and fiscal tightrope.
  • RFK Jr. as a public health threat — from the right: In a notable intra-conservative critique, the WSJ editorial board calls RFK Jr. "dangerous to public health," focusing on his Senate hearing performance and warning that his vaccine skepticism empowers trial lawyers at the expense of sound science.
  • Bondi firing and DOJ leadership: The Federalist argues Trump needs a "vicious operator" to replace Pam Bondi at the Justice Department, contending she was too cautious to pursue the administration's agenda against the "deep state."
  • Easter and Catholic renewal as cultural themes: Multiple right-leaning outlets lean heavily into Easter messaging. Fox News covers Pope Leo XIV's Easter plea against indifference to war, while The Federalist frames American Catholic renewal as a direct rejection of liberal modernity. The Washington Examiner highlights JD Vance's forthcoming Catholic faith memoir as a strategic play for religious voters in 2028.
  • Higher education enforcement: Right-leaning outlets are largely supportive of Trump's demand for college race data, framing it as post-SFFA v. Harvard enforcement. The WSJ opinion page focused on other executive power questions but the broader conservative argument holds that universities are defying the Supreme Court's affirmative action ban and must be held accountable.

What the Left Is Saying

  • The Iran war as an intelligence and strategic catastrophe: The Atlantic's lead analysis argues the war represents a fundamental intelligence failure — the U.S. underestimated Iran's air defenses and overestimated its own ability to control escalation. The Nation blames Netanyahu's war advocacy, arguing Israel pushed the U.S. into a conflict that serves Jerusalem's interests more than Washington's.
  • Immigration enforcement's human toll: The NYT's Texas investigation of children left parentless by ICE raids is being amplified across progressive media. The Guardian reports Uganda has received its first U.S. deportation flight under a new third-country agreement, drawing comparisons to the UK's Rwanda scheme. The Nation profiles immigrant meatpacking workers in Colorado who launched the first major strike in decades, framing their labor action as resistance to both corporate exploitation and federal enforcement.
  • Trump's higher-ed crackdown blocked — for now: Progressive outlets are celebrating a federal judge's injunction blocking Trump's demand for race and GPA data from colleges in 17 states. The ruling is seen as a key check on the administration's effort to weaponize the post-affirmative action landscape to intimidate universities. Forbes tracks the proposed budget cuts to higher education and research, warning of devastating effects on graduate programs and federal grant-funded science.
  • Bondi and Noem firings — incompetence and loyalty: The LA Times' Anita Chabria argues Bondi and Noem were fired not just for incompetence but because Trump's system rewards only servile loyalty and punishes anyone who develops independent standing. The Nation calls Bondi's firing "all too predictable," arguing it was inevitable once she showed any independence.
  • Climate and environment: Vox reports the western U.S. is already running out of water months before summer, with snowpack at historic lows. The New Yorker warns Trump's offshore drilling expansion is "a recipe for poisoning the oceans."
