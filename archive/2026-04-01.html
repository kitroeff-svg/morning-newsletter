Daily Briefing
for Natalie
Wednesday, April 01, 2026

The Supreme Court hears oral arguments today in the most consequential challenge to birthright citizenship since the 14th Amendment was ratified — the first such case in over a century — with the Trump administration arguing that children of undocumented immigrants should not automatically receive U.S. citizenship. Meanwhile, Trump is set to address the nation tonight about the Iran conflict, where his own team is publicly confused about war aims: Axios reports mixed messages on whether the goal is regime change or a limited strike, while oil markets are pricing in a worst-case $200-a-barrel scenario if the Strait of Hormuz stays closed, and gas prices have already jumped $1.08 per gallon in a month. In a striking escalation of the administration's higher-education offensive, a federal judge has ordered the University of Pennsylvania to hand over the names of employees in Jewish affinity groups to the EEOC — a ruling drawing alarm from civil liberties advocates across the political spectrum.

Coming Up

  • Today The Supreme Court hears oral arguments in the birthright citizenship case. Expect a decision by the end of the term in late June, but today's questioning will signal where the conservative supermajority is leaning.
  • Tonight Trump addresses the nation on Iran war plans. The White House has deployed Secretary of State Marco Rubio to 'clarify messaging' ahead of the speech, suggesting internal acknowledgment that the administration's objectives remain muddled.
  • This week Palestinians are streaming back to northern Gaza on foot after a hostage-release breakthrough. Watch for whether the fragile ceasefire holds and how Israel responds to the mass return.
  • Saturday NASA's Artemis II is in its final countdown for a crewed lunar flyby launch — the first astronauts sent beyond low-Earth orbit since Apollo 17 in 1972. Weather and technical reviews continue through the week.
  • Next week First round of April Social Security payments goes out. With gas prices surging and inflation anxieties rising from the Hormuz crisis, expect renewed political pressure on the administration's economic messaging.

What the Right Is Saying

  • Birthright citizenship: National Review frames the case as about the original meaning of 'subject to the jurisdiction thereof' in the 14th Amendment, arguing that the clause was intended to exclude children of foreign nationals without permanent allegiance. The Washington Examiner emphasizes this is the first real test in over 100 years, suggesting Wong Kim Ark was narrower than liberals claim. Axios notes that a ruling for Trump could strip citizenship from millions already born here — a fact the right largely glosses over.
  • Iran war messaging chaos: Even right-leaning outlets acknowledge confusion. The Federalist warns 'the Iran war is splintering' the conservative coalition, with populist-right voices opposing regime change and hawks demanding escalation. The Washington Examiner reports that Rubio has been deployed as a cleanup hitter, and Fox News leads with Trump's threat to leave NATO over allied reluctance — framing it as leverage rather than recklessness. The Examiner's opinion page goes so far as to suggest funding the war by 'ending welfare fraud.'
  • Trump vs. universities — the Penn case: Right-wing media is largely supportive of the federal judge ordering the University of Pennsylvania to hand over names of employees in Jewish affinity groups. National Review frames this as overdue accountability for campus antisemitism. The Federalist, meanwhile, focuses on broader culture-war arguments about SCOTUS and the ideological capture of universities, while Washington Monthly identifies 'bipartisan roots' in some Trump education reforms, noting that Obama-era accountability pushes laid groundwork the current administration has weaponized.
  • WSJ Opinion page: Runs a mix today: praise for Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent's trade balancing act, a surprisingly sharp critique of RFK Jr. as 'dangerous to public health' from their editorial board, and criticism of Trump's lumber tariffs for slowing disaster recovery in fire-ravaged California — a notable intra-right critique.
  • Gas prices and energy: The Examiner notes gas is up $1.08/gallon in a month but avoids blaming the administration directly. Fox Opinion runs a piece arguing red states are 'winning the clean energy race' — a notable shift in tone from the right on renewables.

What the Left Is Saying

  • Gaza and genocide framing: The Nation leads with 'The World Is Watching a Genocide,' a piece that explicitly uses the term and argues the international community's failure to intervene will define a generation. This comes as the WSJ reports Palestinians walking back into devastated northern Gaza and the NYT documents Israel's sectarian evacuation orders in southern Lebanon targeting Shiites specifically.
  • Iran and 'lions led by donkeys': The Atlantic's 'Lions Led by Donkeys' argues that American service members are being sent into a conflict with no coherent strategy, no exit plan, and no honest public case. A companion Atlantic newsletter calls Trump's regime-change rhetoric a 'swerve' from his original limited-strike framing. Vox ties the war directly to Trump's 'gas prices problem,' noting the $1.08/gallon spike is politically devastating for a president who ran on cheap energy.
  • Penn and the list of Jews — alarm from the left: The Guardian frames the court order compelling Penn to provide names of employees in Jewish groups as deeply troubling, drawing parallels to McCarthyist loyalty lists. The core left argument: the government is using antisemitism investigations as a pretext to compile identity-based lists at universities, chilling free association. The Atlantic's broader piece on Trump's higher-ed assault 'hitting a snag' notes that legal challenges are mounting, but the chilling effect on campuses is already real.
  • PEPFAR and HIV funding gutted: Vox runs a major two-part package warning that the 45-year fight against HIV — 'one of humanity's greatest victories' — is being undermined by PEPFAR and USAID cuts. They argue the administration's defunding could reverse decades of progress that saves an estimated 25 million lives. The Atlantic reports HHS officials have spent a 'year in purgatory' being sidelined.
  • Domestic politics: The Nation mocks House Republicans' 'bottomless stupidity' on DHS funding, while Vox makes the case that paid parental leave is more politically winnable than progressives realize.

Supreme Court & Constitutional Law

  • Supreme Court Takes Up Birthright Citizenship for First Time in Over a CenturyNYT The justices will hear arguments on whether the 14th Amendment's guarantee of citizenship to all born on U.S. soil applies to children of undocumented immigrants. The Trump administration's challenge could affect an estimated 4.4 million U.S.-born citizens with unauthorized parents. The case hasn't been revisited since United States v. Wong Kim Ark in 1898.
  • The Man Who Won Birthright Citizenship — Whose Family Never KnewNYT A deeply reported NYT feature traces the descendants of Wong Kim Ark, the Chinese-American cook whose 1898 Supreme Court case established birthright citizenship. His own family was unaware of his legal legacy, a story that underscores how personal immigration histories shape the court's current deliberations.
  • Judge Orders University of Pennsylvania to Hand Over List of Jewish Employees to EEOCNYT / Multiple A federal judge approved the Trump administration's effort to compel Penn to turn over the names of employees who belong to Jewish affinity groups as part of an EEOC investigation into antisemitism complaints. Critics say the subpoena is a chilling use of civil rights enforcement as a weapon against elite universities; supporters argue the school has been uncooperative on documented antisemitism.
