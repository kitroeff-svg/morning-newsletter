Sunday, May 03, 2026

The Iran war dominates Sunday's coverage: Trump faces cratering public support for the conflict while threatening further strikes if Tehran "misbehaves," and The Atlantic frames the war as a defining midterm issue for both parties. The Supreme Court's gutting of the Voting Rights Act is drawing fierce reactions across the ideological spectrum — the New Yorker calls it a demolition, while the Congressional Black Caucus faces what the Washington Examiner terms a "lose-lose" situation. In Gaza, Palestinians are streaming back to northern territories on foot after a hostage-release breakthrough, even as freed Israeli hostages are still being treated for shrapnel from the Oct. 7 attack. Meanwhile, NATO has activated its first coordinated response to suspected deep-sea cable sabotage, and the Trump administration is accelerating its crackdown on higher education with new caps on graduate school loans and plans to strip accreditation from programs it deems insufficiently career-oriented.

What the Right Is Saying

  • The WHCD assassination attempt continues to dominate right-wing commentary. National Review argues that the left "just can't process" the attack on its own terms, while a separate NR piece condemns what it calls runaway "political-violence whataboutism" that equates the attempt with other incidents. Fox News focuses on accused gunman Cole Allen's psychological profile, with experts calling his writings evidence of a "scattered" mindset.
  • Trump fires the National Science Board. National Review reports approvingly on Trump's dismissal of the entire NSB, framing it as part of a broader effort to dismantle what the right sees as a politicized scientific establishment. This dovetails with the administration's higher-education crackdown: Trump wants to cut federal loans for college programs in music, arts, and education that he deems insufficiently career-oriented, per Fortune, and plans sweeping accreditation changes that could strip federal aid from programs the administration considers low-value.
  • WSJ Opinion defends Trump on trade and TikTok. One column argues Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent is skillfully managing tariff policy, while another defends Trump's TikTok extension as constitutionally sound. However, a third piece criticizes lumber tariffs as counterproductive for disaster recovery, especially for California wildfire rebuilding.
  • WSJ Opinion breaks with Trump on RFK Jr. A pointed column argues that RFK Jr. is "dangerous to public health," citing his Senate hearing performance and his ties to trial lawyers undermining vaccine policy — a notable dissent from the Wall Street Journal's opinion page.
  • Midterm positioning. The Washington Examiner frames the midterm landscape as challenging but navigable for Republicans, arguing that the GOP "sees a path through the map" despite Democratic leads in early polling. Rep. Randy Fine faces backlash after saying "We don't want Armenians" in Congress.

What the Left Is Saying

  • The Supreme Court's VRA ruling is being treated as a democratic emergency. The New Yorker frames the decision as the culmination of a decades-long conservative legal campaign that has now "demolished" the landmark law. The Nation reports that "Republicans can't contain their glee" and argues the ruling will suppress minority turnout ahead of the midterms. A companion Nation piece examines the role of conservative amicus briefs in shaping the outcome.
  • The Iran war and DOJ aggression. Vox scrutinizes Trump's claim that the war is "over" while strikes continue, calling it a rhetorical strategy to sidestep congressional war powers. A separate Vox video report frames Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth's rhetoric as quasi-religious, calling his Pentagon "The Department of Holy War." The Atlantic warns the DOJ has entered a "new, even more aggressive phase" of political prosecutions.
  • Abortion pill access restricted. Axios reports the appeals court ruling limiting mifepristone access, and The Nation frames the Fifth Circuit's decision as an attempt to "unilaterally reimpose an outdated protocol" that would force patients to make unnecessary in-person visits, effectively rolling back FDA standards based on ideology rather than science.
  • DNC autopsy suppression and party dysfunction. The Nation asks why DNC Chair Ken Martin is reportedly "covering up" the party's 2024 autopsy, arguing that internal accountability is being sacrificed to protect Democratic leadership from embarrassment — a pattern that hampers rebuilding.
  • Higher education under siege. Progressive outlets note the Trump administration's new caps on graduate school loans and its refusal to classify education degrees as "professional" — moves that Education Week warns could devastate teacher preparation programs nationwide. The Guardian reports Zambia's cancellation of the world's largest human rights and tech summit, read by some as a chilling effect of authoritarian-friendly diplomacy.
  • Global democratic backsliding. The Guardian flags Uganda's new anti-dissent bill as modeled on Russian and Chinese repression, while The Atlantic declares that "the era of rational discourse is over," invoking Jürgen Habermas to argue that the conditions for democratic debate have collapsed under Trump.
