for Natalie
Tuesday, March 31, 2026

The Iran crisis is reaching an inflection point: the NYT reports Trump faces a pivotal decision on whether to launch a ground war, even as Iran's fractured leadership struggles to coordinate a response and Pakistan emerges as an unlikely mediator. The war's economic toll is landing at the pump — U.S. gas prices have hit $4 a gallon on average, the highest since 2022, while European inflation ticked up to 2.5% on surging energy costs. In Gaza, Palestinians are streaming back to northern neighborhoods on foot after a hostage-release breakthrough, with freed Israeli hostages still carrying shrapnel from October 7 in their bodies. And NATO has activated its first coordinated response to suspected deep-sea cable sabotage, a significant escalation in the alliance's posture toward hybrid threats.

What the Right Is Saying

  • War fatigue and the base: National Review warns that Trump's populist base — many of whom supported him on an anti-interventionist platform — will ultimately weigh in on the Iran escalation. The piece argues the White House can't indefinitely ignore grassroots skepticism about a ground war, and that the political cost of $4 gas is compounding the problem.
  • Hegseth's 'unshackled warfare': Axios reports that Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth is pursuing what it calls an 'era of unshackled warfare,' with fewer constraints on targeting and rules of engagement. The Atlantic's newsletter argues Hegseth is 'vice signaling' — deliberately broadcasting harshness as a political brand. Right-wing outlets are largely supportive, while the Washington Examiner highlights Trump showing video of massive bunker-buster strikes.
  • Scott Bessent and trade policy: The WSJ editorial board praises Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent as a stabilizing force capable of navigating Trump's tariff agenda without crashing markets. Separately, the Journal's editorial page criticizes Trump's lumber tariffs for making disaster recovery more expensive, calling it a self-inflicted wound.
  • RFK Jr. and public health: WSJ Opinion argues RFK Jr. is 'dangerous to public health,' criticizing his HHS stewardship and ties to trial lawyers. Meanwhile, The Atlantic reports the MAHA (Make America Healthy Again) agenda has been given an 'impossible task' — sidelined by Iran war spending and political bandwidth.
  • Beijing and AI: National Review warns about Beijing's 'hostage strategy' in the AI race, arguing China is leveraging control over rare-earth minerals and data access to constrain American AI development. The piece calls for aggressive decoupling.
  • Higher education: Minding the Campus argues Trump's DEI rollback at universities is 'failing,' claiming that despite executive orders, most institutions have simply renamed diversity offices rather than eliminated them. The piece calls for enforceable funding conditions. The University of North Texas slashing dozens of programs is viewed on the right as a natural consequence of bloated higher-ed budgets.
  • ICE and immigration enforcement: Fox News leads with outrage over a local jurisdiction ignoring an ICE detainer in a case connected to the killing of a 24-year-old mother, framing it as proof that sanctuary policies endanger Americans.

What the Left Is Saying

  • 'Are we the baddies?': The Nation directly asks what Americans' moral obligations are 'when your country is the villain,' framing the Iran war as an illegal, unjust conflict. Bernie Sanders delivered a speech at the 'No Kings' rally in St. Paul demanding 'this war must end immediately,' drawing large crowds that The Nation describes as combining 'joy, healing, and resolve.'
  • Hegseth's 'holy war': The Nation's Jeet Heer calls Hegseth's framing of the Iran campaign an 'unholy nightmare,' arguing the defense secretary is importing Christian nationalist ideology into military strategy. The New Yorker examines the 'spectacle of war' and how the administration's media strategy — including Trump's bunker-buster video — is designed to make protest feel futile.
  • DHS shutdown and GOP dysfunction: The Nation calls House Republicans' handling of DHS funding 'bottomless stupidity,' noting TSA workers are going unpaid while Congress fights over immigration enforcement riders. Vox explains the convoluted mechanism Trump is using to partially pay TSA — an arrangement that is legally dubious and practically insufficient.
  • Rahm Emanuel's counter-programming: Axios reports Rahm Emanuel is proposing to divert ICE funding to community colleges, a provocative bid to reframe the immigration debate around economic investment. It signals Democratic messaging strategy heading into the 2026 midterms.
  • Social media and democratic erosion: An Axios-reported poll finds that 'super scrollers' — the heaviest social media users — are significantly more likely to sour on democracy than light users. Progressive commentators are citing the data as evidence that platform regulation is urgent.
  • Higher education under siege: Times Higher Education reports the U.S. Education Department is 'not dead but being dismantled' under Trump. A multi-state lawsuit argues that Trump's college data mandate threatens student privacy, while The Atlantic reports Trump's assault on higher education 'has hit a snag' — nearly all universities rejected his restrictive Compact for Academic Excellence.
  • World Cup and immigration: The New Yorker reports that international World Cup players are navigating Trump's immigration crackdown, with some fearing visa complications and detentions — an intersection of sports and politics that could generate significant global attention as the tournament approaches.
