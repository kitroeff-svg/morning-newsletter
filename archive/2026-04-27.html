Daily Briefing
for Natalie
Monday, April 27, 2026

The aftermath of Saturday night's shooting at the White House Correspondents' Dinner dominates Monday's news: authorities say suspect Cole Allen, driven by political grievance, traveled cross-country to target Trump officials at the Washington Hilton, and the attack is already reshaping Secret Service protocols, with chief of staff Susie Wiles convening a review meeting. Overseas, Iran has floated a deal to reopen the Strait of Hormuz while postponing nuclear talks — a move that failed to calm markets, with oil prices rising and stocks wavering as the U.S.-Iran conflict drags on global growth. In Gaza, Palestinians are streaming back to northern areas on foot after a hostage-release breakthrough, even as freed Israeli hostages are found still carrying shrapnel from the October 7 attack. And in Africa, crises are multiplying: Rwanda-backed M23 rebels have entered a key safe-haven city in Congo, Mali's defense minister has been killed in coordinated insurgent attacks, and a Colombian bus bombing ahead of May elections has killed at least 20.

Coming Up

  • This week Susie Wiles will hold a White House meeting on Secret Service protocols at Trump events following the WHCD shooting. Expect significant changes to how senior officials are protected at non-governmental gatherings.
  • This week The Supreme Court takes up two major cases: geofence warrants (a key law enforcement surveillance tool) and the Roundup herbicide litigation, the latter with direct implications for RFK Jr.'s MAHA agenda and federal preemption of state tort claims.
  • Coming days Watch for Iran's Strait of Hormuz proposal to gain or lose traction. Tehran is offering to reopen the strait in exchange for delaying nuclear talks — a split-the-difference gambit that could ease energy markets or collapse entirely.
  • May elections Colombia heads to elections in May amid escalating violence. The bus bombing on the Pan-American Highway that killed 20 is the most lethal attack in years, and cocaine-funded gangs are destabilizing the country years after the FARC peace pact.
  • Ongoing NATO is conducting its first-ever coordinated response to suspected sabotage of a deep-sea communications cable, a significant escalation in the gray-zone warfare threat that allied nations have been warning about.
