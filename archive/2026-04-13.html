Monday, April 13, 2026

The Middle East dominates this Monday: the U.S. is preparing to blockade the Strait of Hormuz as Iran nuclear talks sputter, sending oil prices surging and raising recession alarms — the Washington Examiner notes 10 of 12 postwar recessions were preceded by crude price spikes. In Europe, Viktor Orbán has been swept from power after 16 years, a stunning rebuke to the illiberal model that had made him a MAGA hero; Peter Magyar's opposition coalition won decisively. Trump escalated his feud with Pope Leo XIV, posting a Jesus-like image of himself on Truth Social after calling the new pope 'weak on crime' and 'terrible on foreign policy' — the pontiff, unfazed, departs today for a landmark tour of four African nations. In Gaza, Palestinians are streaming back to northern neighborhoods on foot after a hostage-release breakthrough, while freed Israeli hostages are revealed to still carry shrapnel in their bodies from the Oct. 7 attack.

What the Right Is Saying

  • Trump vs. Pope Leo XIV: Fox News and the Washington Examiner covered Trump's broadside calling Pope Leo 'weak on crime' and 'terrible on foreign policy', but the right-leaning coverage notably didn't rally behind the Jesus-like Truth Social image. Fox reported the pope's calm response — 'unafraid' — largely without editorializing against the pontiff, suggesting even friendly outlets see risk in this feud.
  • Oil and the economy: The Washington Examiner sounded a genuine alarm, noting that 10 of 12 postwar recessions were preceded by oil price spikes — an implicit warning to the administration about the economic cost of the Hormuz blockade threat. WSJ Opinion took a more soothing tack, arguing that Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent can 'walk the trade tightrope' and manage market volatility.
  • Public health and RFK Jr.: A WSJ Opinion editorial declared RFK Jr. 'dangerous to public health,' citing his Senate hearing performance and ties to trial lawyers — a notable break from the administration line by the Journal's editorial board. Meanwhile, Fox reported on rising patient demands for 'unvaccinated' blood, framing it as a grassroots movement while quoting doctors warning of health risks.
  • Melania and Epstein: Fox highlighted Melania Trump's remarks about the Epstein files, arguing they put pressure on Acting AG Todd Blanche to accelerate DOJ action on disclosure — a rare instance of Fox framing a Trump family member as pressuring the Trump administration itself.
  • Higher education and DEI: National Review argued that 'DEI abuses are still happening' despite executive orders, claiming universities are merely rebranding diversity programs. This dovetails with reporting that Arizona universities quietly altered DEI offerings after Trump threatened funding cuts — but refused to say how. The right sees this as proof the pressure is working but isn't going far enough.
  • Lumber tariffs: The WSJ editorial board warned that Trump's lumber tariffs on Canada are directly raising the cost of rebuilding after the California fires — a pointed critique from a free-trade perspective.

What the Left Is Saying

  • Orbán's fall as a turning point: Vox declared 'Viktor Orbán has fallen' and framed the result as a direct rebuttal to those who argued illiberal democracy was an unstoppable global trend. The Atlantic's companion piece, 'Illiberalism Is Not Inevitable,' argued the Hungary result should embolden democratic movements from Poland to the U.S. Peter Magyar's victory is being treated as the most significant pro-democracy electoral result in Europe since Poland's 2023 election.
  • Democracy at home: Vox reported on new data suggesting Trump's 'assault on democracy may be stalling out,' citing resistance from courts, states, and civil society. The Nation pointed to Wisconsin's recent court election as a 'glimpse of a post-Trump future,' arguing progressive judicial wins signal enduring backlash.
  • Family detention returns: The New Yorker published a major investigation into the return of family detention under the current administration, detailing conditions at facilities and the legal challenges mounting against the policy. The piece draws explicit parallels to the Obama-era Dilley and Karnes centers.
  • Higher education under siege: Progressive outlets are tracking multiple fronts. The Conversation linked the Harvard and UCLA lawsuits to a decades-old ideological fight over civil rights law, arguing the DOJ suits represent a radical reinterpretation. The Atlantic reported that Trump's crackdown on higher education 'has hit a snag,' with courts blocking funding freezes and universities finding legal workarounds. Times Higher Education warned of a 'chilling effect' on science from Trump's funding cuts — even if reversed.
  • AI and inequality: Vox's provocative report on OpenAI 'pivoting to socialism' examines Sam Altman's new super PAC pushing economic redistribution policies — a move critics call self-serving PR to forestall regulation. Axios separately describes an emerging three-way split in public attitudes: AI elites, AI skeptics, and AI doomers.
  • Trump family profiteering: The Seattle Times reported that the Trump family's ongoing deal spree 'could open the door for future presidents to profit from office,' cataloging recent licensing and investment arrangements that test the boundaries of presidential ethics norms.
