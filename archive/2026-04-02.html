What the Right Is Saying

  • Iran war: Mission accomplished, sort of. The Federalist ran with Trump's framing that "war objectives have been achieved" and Iran is "no longer a threat," while Fox News emphasized Trump's call for the world to "build delayed courage" and seize the Strait of Hormuz from Iranian influence. National Review's take was more cautious, asking whether a "Hormuz Handoff" to regional allies is strategically viable or a recipe for chaos.
  • Birthright citizenship: defiance of SCOTUS. The Federalist published multiple pieces attacking the Court's ruling, with one arguing that the "same Constitution" would never authorize birthright citizenship for children of unauthorized immigrants, and another warning that the Court upholds birthright citizenship "at its own peril." This is a notable escalation in right-media willingness to challenge SCOTUS legitimacy.
  • DHS shutdown resolution. Fox News framed the DHS funding deal as the House GOP "caving" to Senate demands, a rare instance of right-leaning media acknowledging intra-party dysfunction rather than blaming Democrats.
  • RFK Jr. and public health. WSJ Opinion published a pointed critique arguing RFK Jr. is "dangerous to public health," citing his Senate hearing performance and ties to trial lawyers — a notable break from populist-right support for Kennedy's HHS agenda.
  • Trade and tariffs. WSJ Opinion ran a defense of Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent's ability to "walk the trade tightrope," while separately warning that Trump's lumber tariffs are actively hurting disaster recovery in California — a tension within the pro-Trump economic argument.
  • Higher education. The Washington Examiner highlighted a major teachers union funding anti-ICE protests at public schools, framing it as evidence of institutional left-wing activism in education. Inside Higher Ed reported that proposed federal funding strings are seen as "potentially existential" by universities, a framing conservatives welcome as overdue accountability.

What the Left Is Saying

  • Trump's Iran speech was strategically incoherent. The Atlantic's analysis, titled "Maybe Trump Should Not Have Given This Speech," argued the address simultaneously declared victory and promised weeks more bombing — a contradiction that undermines allied confidence and domestic credibility. Vox asked whether this marks "the beginning of the end" of the Iran war or merely a rhetorical pivot masking indefinite escalation.
  • Birthright citizenship win. The New Yorker detailed the "long odds" of ever overturning birthright citizenship, framing the Court's rejection as a significant constitutional firewall. The Atlantic called Trump's arguments "absurd" and noted they "went nowhere" — a rare clear-cut legal defeat for the administration.
  • Mail-in voting under attack. Vox reported on Trump's new executive order targeting mail-in voting ahead of the midterm elections, framing it as a voter suppression tactic wrapped in election integrity language.
  • Venezuela sanctions relief. The Guardian reported the U.S. lifted sanctions on Venezuela's acting president Delcy Rodríguez — a quietly significant diplomatic move that could signal a broader realignment of Latin America policy.
  • Supreme Court and conversion therapy. The Nation published a forceful condemnation of the Court's counseling decision, arguing it "condemned countless kids to psychiatric abuse" by striking down conversion therapy bans on First Amendment grounds. National Review celebrated the same ruling as a victory for free speech.
  • Silicon Valley anti-intellectualism. The Nation's deep dive on Peter Thiel and Marc Andreessen argued that tech billionaires' embrace of populist politics is rooted in a genuine "anti-intellectualism" hostile to universities and expertise — relevant context as Trump's higher-ed crackdown hits legal snags but universities brace for proposed funding conditions called "potentially existential."
