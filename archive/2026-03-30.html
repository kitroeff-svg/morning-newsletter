What the Right Is Saying

  • CPAC rallies behind Trump and Iran action. A Fox News/CPAC straw poll shows Trump dominating conservative support, with attendees broadly endorsing military action against Iran. The base appears unified on the war, even as cracks appear elsewhere in the MAGA coalition — The Atlantic reports the manosphere is turning on Trump over the conflict.
  • Rand Paul floats 2028 presidential bid, signals anti-war lane. Sen. Rand Paul told Fox News he's '50-50' on running for president in 2028, positioning himself as the libertarian, non-interventionist alternative in what could be a crowded Republican field.
  • WSJ editorial board defends Bessent and trade policy. A WSJ Opinion piece argues Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent is skillfully managing the tension between Trump's tariff agenda and market stability. Separately, the board warns that Trump's lumber tariffs are raising costs for disaster recovery in California fire zones — a rare internal tension in conservative trade arguments.
  • RFK Jr. called 'dangerous to public health.' The WSJ editorial board published a sharp critique of HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., arguing his vaccine skepticism and ties to trial lawyers make him a genuine threat to public health outcomes — a notable attack from a right-leaning outlet on a Trump appointee.
  • Higher education: Trump investigates medical school admissions. The Trump administration has begun inquiries into admission policies at three medical schools, probing for potential violations of the administration's anti-DEI executive orders. National Review and The Federalist have largely cheered the broader crackdown on universities, with The Federalist framing Democratic opposition to various Trump proposals as evidence of progressive bad faith.
  • ICE at airports and polls. Sen. James Lankford called Democrats' demand for no ICE presence at polling places 'absurd' during DHS reform negotiations. The Federalist blasted 'weak Senate Republicans' for endangering the mass deportation agenda.

What the Left Is Saying

  • 'Even John Bolton is against this war.' Progressive media is almost uniformly opposed to the Iran escalation. Vox highlights that even John Bolton — the arch-hawk — has criticized Trump's Iran campaign, arguing it lacks strategic coherence. The Nation declares 'the politicians won't stop this war — only the people can,' framing the 'No Kings' protests as the locus of anti-war energy.
  • The religious right fractures over Israel and Iran. Vox reports a significant split within the religious right, with Catholic conservatives and some evangelicals breaking from the reflexive pro-Israel, pro-war consensus. The piece traces the theological roots of the divide — dispensationalist Zionism vs. Catholic just-war tradition — and names Tucker Carlson and Ambassador Mike Huckabee as figures on opposite sides.
  • TSA meltdown and the failing state. The New Yorker's investigation into the TSA crisis traces it to cascading budget cuts, DOGE-adjacent staffing mandates, and political indifference. The Nation's Elie Mystal argues bluntly that 'nothing works in Trump's America — except racism,' connecting airport chaos, ICE deployments, and the administration's priorities.
  • Cuba: the human cost of the blockade. The Guardian emphasizes the humanitarian framing of the Cuba blockade story, while progressive outlets point to NYT reporting that Cuban patients are dying because hospitals cannot run generators or refrigerate medicine. The decision to allow one Russian tanker through is framed as too little, too late.
  • Higher education under siege. Progressive outlets are alarmed by Trump's expanding crackdown on universities. Times Higher Education reports the Education Department is 'not dead but being dismantled.' Jewish faculty at Harvard have spoken out against Trump's attacks, and a multi-state lawsuit argues Trump's college data mandate threatens student privacy. The Atlantic notes that Trump's assault on higher education has 'hit a snag' — almost all universities rejected his restrictive 'Compact for Academic Excellence.'
  • Courts crack down on Big Tech's harm to children. Vox reports that courts are finally issuing rulings punishing Meta and Google for harms to minors, but warns the legal victories could create free-speech complications. A separate ruling on hate content could set broader precedent for platform liability.
